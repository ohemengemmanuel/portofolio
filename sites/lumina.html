Technology

From filament to photon

The science of artificial light has evolved over 145 years — from Edison's carbon filament glowing at 2400K, to quantum-dot LEDs approaching 200 lumens per watt with perfect colour rendering.

  • Incandescent — thermal radiation, 2700K
  • LED — electroluminescence, 80–200 lm/W
  • Neon — gas discharge at 15,000–18,000V
  • Chandelier — optics via lead crystal refraction
  • Floodlight — high-lumen area illumination

History

145 years of artificial light

1802

Humphry Davy's arc lamp

The English chemist demonstrated the first electric arc light using 2,000 battery cells — a blinding, impractical, magnificent beginning. It proved electricity could produce light, igniting a century of innovation.

Arc Discharge
1879

Edison's carbon filament bulb

Thomas Edison patented a practical incandescent bulb using a carbonised bamboo filament that lasted 1,200 hours. The era of domestic electric lighting began. The warm 2700K glow of the incandescent became the benchmark humanity would measure all artificial light against.

Incandescent
1910

Tungsten filament & neon tubes

William Coolidge's tungsten filament dramatically improved bulb lifespan. That same decade, Georges Claude displayed the first neon lamp at the Paris Motor Show — a vivid crimson tube that would define the visual identity of the 20th century city.

Incandescent · Neon
1938

Fluorescent lighting arrives

General Electric introduced the first commercial fluorescent tubes. Offices and factories would never look the same. Cool, efficient, and clinical — it traded the warmth of incandescent for economy. The fluorescent tube became shorthand for institutional light.

Fluorescent
1962

Nick Holonyak Jr. invents the LED

At General Electric, Holonyak created the first visible-spectrum LED — a dim red point of light. He predicted it would one day replace the incandescent bulb. It took 50 years, but he was right. The LED now accounts for over 50% of global lighting sales.

LED
1993

Blue LED — completing the spectrum

Shuji Nakamura achieved the high-brightness blue LED at Nichia Corporation — the missing piece. Combining blue with yellow phosphor produced white light. Nakamura, Akasaki, and Amano won the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physics. The modern lighting revolution was complete.

LED · Nobel Prize
2008

LED strips & flexible light

The miniaturisation of surface-mount LEDs made continuous flexible strips possible — light could now bend around corners, run under cabinets, outline architecture, and be cut to any length. Lighting became truly spatial and three-dimensional.

LED Strip
2015

Smart lighting & tunable white

Connected bulbs from Philips Hue, LIFX, and others brought full colour spectrum control to consumers. Circadian lighting — automatically shifting from 2700K warmth at dawn to 5000K daylight at noon — became available in homes. Light was no longer static.

Smart · Circadian
Today

200 lm/W and beyond

Laboratory LEDs have surpassed 300 lumens per watt. Quantum-dot technology enables perfect colour rendering at Ra 99+. OLEDs bring diffuse, shadow-free panel light. Micro-LEDs promise displays and illumination from a single substrate. The journey from Edison's bamboo filament continues.

Quantum Dot · OLED · MicroLED

Buying guide

How to choose the right light

Match your room's purpose

A reading nook demands a warm, directional light around 2700–3000K. A kitchen benefits from cool 4000K task lighting. A bathroom needs high CRI (90+) to render skin tones accurately. Always consider what activity happens in the space.

Rule of thumb: warm for rest, cool for work, neutral for versatility.

Lumens, not watts

Watts measure energy consumption; lumens measure actual light output. A 10W LED can produce the same 800 lumens as a 60W incandescent. For general living spaces, aim for 300–500 lm/m². For task areas, go up to 750–1000 lm/m².

800 lm replaces a 60W bulb. 1600 lm replaces a 100W bulb.

CRI — Colour Rendering Index

CRI measures how accurately a light source reveals colours compared to natural sunlight (Ra 100). For homes, Ra 90+ is ideal. Retail and art require Ra 95+. Avoid anything below Ra 80 — colours will look washed out and skin will appear pallid.

Good LEDs now achieve Ra 97–98 at reasonable cost.

Dimming compatibility

Not all LEDs dim smoothly. Look for "dimmable" certification and pair with a trailing-edge dimmer (not leading-edge) for flicker-free operation. Warm-dim LEDs are a premium option that shift from 3000K to 1800K as dimmed, mimicking incandescent behaviour.

Test at 10% brightness — cheap dimmers flicker visibly.

Beam angle matters

Spotlights typically have 10–25° beams for accent and artwork. Pendants and table lamps spread at 90–120° for ambient. Floodlights cover 120°+. Layering narrow accents with wide ambient sources creates the depth and drama of professional interior lighting.

Layer three types: ambient, task, and accent.

IP rating for wet areas

IP (Ingress Protection) ratings define water and dust resistance. IP44 is the minimum for bathroom zones 2. IP65 is required in shower zones (zone 1). Outdoor fixtures should be IP65 or higher. Never install an unrated interior fixture in a bathroom — it is a legal and safety issue.

IP65 = dust-tight + protected against jets of water.
